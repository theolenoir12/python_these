Editor and Reviewer comments:  
 


AE: The topic falls in the scope of the journal, but most reviewers do not recommend the publication of the paper. The reviewers have raised serious concerns on the novelty and clarity of the study.


Reviewer #1: This paper proposes a rule-based energy management strategy for microgrids considering lifetime degradation to improve component service life. The overall novelty is limited; there are already many similar studies on lifetime degradation, and the rule-based management logic shows no significant new ideas.
1. The introduction is overly lengthy. Some basic content is unnecessary and should be condensed to maintain the conciseness expected of academic papers. Moreover, the overall writing style is verbose and reads more like a project technical report. It is recommended to refine the text and revise it in the style of a scientific paper.
2. The main body presents theoretical methods; therefore, specific case-study parameters should not be provided here. Table 2 (parameter table) should be moved to the case analysis section.
3. For the small-scale microgrid described in the paper, online optimization management does not require significant computational power, so rule-based control does not offer a clear advantage.
4. Figure 4 is insufficient to illustrate the detailed generation process of the specific rule-based control commands. The energy management workflow should be improved and clarified.
5. The designed rule-based energy management strategy is overly simplified and does not address dynamic power allocation issues. In practice, the power response of fuel cells and batteries involves dynamic delay processes, meaning that the given commands will not be tracked instantly. Furthermore, what is the converter control method for each component under the proposed control strategy? Which component is responsible for stabilizing the DC bus voltage?
6. There should be a unified quantitative metric for residential electricity loss and aging loss, for example, converting both into economic losses for trade-off analysis. Although aging extends the lifespan of components such as batteries, it comes at the cost of residential electricity rights. Would directly replacing the aged battery components be more cost-effective than incurring such losses?



Reviewer #2: This manuscript presents a framework for health-conscious energy management in hybrid PV-Battery-Hydrogen microgrids. The research attempt to address a critical tension between short-term power reliability and long-term economic sustainability. By integrating degradation indicators into an adaptive rule-based energy management strategy, the authors provide a practical tool for microgrid operators. The 15-year simulation horizon is a significant strength. The Pareto analysis also quantifies the trade-off. However, the macroscopic nature of the model raises concerns regarding its fidelity. The following are the detailed comments.

1- An LPSP increase of 12.8% is substantial. The authors should discuss the social acceptability of this trade-off for residential users.
2- The 1-hour step (Line 517) is quite coarse. How does this impact the accuracy of the d_transient term in Eqs. 9 and 10? Rapid fluctuations within the hour could accelerate aging significantly. Add some discussion in the limitation section.
3- In a macroscopic 1-hour model, how is a "start-stop event" for the PEMFC defined?
4- Regarding to Eq. 14, the adaptation uses a linear scaling of power offsets by SoH. Is there a physical or experimental basis for this linear relationship?
5- Does the model account for the decrease in energy conversion efficiency as components age? Reduced efficiency would lead to higher hydrogen consumption and potentially higher LPSP over time.
6- The study assumes SoH estimation is very accurate. Please add a brief sensitivity analysis, how would a ±10% error in SoH estimation affect the performance of the adaptive EMS?
7- Does the load/PV profile in Figure 2 incorporate seasonal differences
8- Consider adding a row for "This Work" in Table 1 to explicitly highlight the multi-decade/multi-storage gap you are filling.
9- Is there any way to validate the macroscopic degradation rates used in the simulation?
10- Figures 7 and 8 compress 15 years of data into a single view, making it impossible to see micro-level adaptive actions.
11- Authors can consider to add a subsection discussing the generalizability of the framework. For example, how would the Pareto-optimal trade-offs shift in colder climates or regions with higher solar intermittency?



Reviewer #3: This manuscript proposes a methodological framework for energy management in an isolated PV-battery-hydrogen DC microgrid on La Réunion island. Nine rule-based EMS are compared in a two-objective Pareto plane (LPSP vs. cost-weighted degradation), and the most balanced one (RB2) is enhanced with an SoH-feedback layer (RB2-SoH) that scales the fuel-cell and electrolyzer power offsets by their estimated state-of-health. The case study is realistic, the writing is clear, and the dual-indicator framework is sensible. However, several methodological issues need to be addressed before the contribution can be regarded as well-supported.
Major comments
(1) The contribution of the "adaptive layer" is not isolated. RB2-SoH differs from RB2 in two ways simultaneously: time-varying behavior and lower average hydrogen-side power. Without a control comparison, it is impossible to tell whether the −34% degradation reduction comes from adaptivity or simply from operating at a lower fixed offset. Please add at least one static low-power RB2 variant (e.g. with offsets fixed at 70% and 85% of the nominal values) so that the adaptivity itself can be evaluated.
(2) No literature benchmark is included. Section 1.2.2 cites MPC-based (K/bidi, Hou) and RL-based (Han, Ren, Liu) EMS, but the Section 3 comparison contains only the authors' own rule designs. A claim that RB2-SoH offers a favorable trade-off requires comparison against at least one representative method from the cited literature, even in simplified form.
(3) The degradation models contain several simplifications presented without sensitivity analysis, and all results are point estimates from a single deterministic run. Specifically: (i) PEMFC/PEMWE degradation is a linear superposition of four mechanisms with seemingly arbitrary thresholds (1%, 80%, 60%); (ii) battery calendar aging is excluded on the grounds that the proposed indicator targets mechanisms "directly influenced by energy management decisions," but SoC residence time is exactly such an EMS-controlled variable, so the exclusion is not well justified; (iii) the battery EoL criterion of 40% capacity loss is on the high side for typical stationary battery applications (20-30% is more commonly used) and is not justified in the text. Please add a sensitivity scan over the dominant parameters (C-weights, the four PEM thresholds, EoL thresholds, inclusion of calendar aging), reported with uncertainty bands on the Pareto plane.
(4) There is internal tension between the hourly sampling and the conclusion that start-stop dominates degradation (94.5% for PEMFC, 56.6% for PEMWE). Sub-hourly start-stop events are aggregated away by the 1 h step, so this dominance may partly be an artifact of how start-stop is counted. A short comparison with a finer time step (e.g. 5 min) over a representative subset of the horizon would help quantify this bias.
(5) The sizing (PV ≈ 32.7 kW for a 2.2 kW average load, FC = 1.5 kW < peak load 4.2 kW) heavily over-sizes PV/ELY and under-sizes FC, which structurally forces battery-heavy strategies to outperform hydrogen-heavy ones. The provenance of this sizing should be explained, and the robustness of the EMS ranking to sizing variations should be at least qualitatively discussed; otherwise the Pareto ordering in Fig. 6 cannot be generalized.

Minor comments
(1) The "+12.8% LPSP" headline is misleading without absolute values. The RB2 baseline appears to sit at a low LPSP value in Fig. 6, so a 12.8% relative increase likely corresponds to a small absolute change. Please report both relative and absolute values in the abstract and in Section 3.2.
(2) Sections 2.3.1 and 2.3.2 describe SoH estimation by internal-resistance methods and EKF, but the simulations assume SoH is known accurately. Please either inject reasonable estimation noise (e.g. ±2% Gaussian) and report robustness, or reposition Section 2.3 as future implementation considerations to avoid implying it has been validated in this study.


Reviewer #4: 1. Lines 36-57: This section requires specific references to support technical claims.
2. Table 1: The table currently lacks a unique value and repeats information found in the text. Either expand it to include more studies and specifications or remove it.
3. The introduction does not state that multiple energy management systems will be compared. Please update the objectives to explicitly include this comparative analysis to ensure the scope is clear from the start.
4. Clarify how the PV profile was generated. If you used simulation, provide the specific modeling parameters: solar irradiation data source (e.g., Global Solar Atlas 2025), tilt angle, and Global Horizontal Irradiation (GHI) values.
5. The battery and hydrogen storage models must be validated. Please include a comparison with experimental data or high-fidelity simulation benchmarks to confirm the accuracy.
6. Line 294: It is unclear how the control is optimized. Specify the optimization method used.
7. Section 2.6: Elaborate on the specific logic, control loops, or algorithms of the EMSs employed.
8. Explain the integration between online estimation and the physical models. Specifically, clarify the mathematical relationship used to estimate internal resistance from the State of Charge (SoC) model—for instance, using a Dual Extended Kalman Filter (DEKF) or Recursive Least Squares (RLS) (ThaiScience, 2026).
9. Fig. 8: your results show batteries reaching 60% SoH quickly (around 5 years). However, modern Li-ion (specifically LiFePO4) batteries are rated for 10-15 years and 6,000+ cycles before significant degradation. Please explain this discrepancy.
10. In residential applications, the standard "End of Life" is typically 70-80% SoH. Please justify why 60% was chosen as the operational limit for this study.
